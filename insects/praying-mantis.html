the body

these image by pinterest.com

By any name, these fascinating insects are formidable predators. They have triangular heads poised on a long "neck," or elongated thorax. (1)

Mantids can turn their heads 180 degrees to scan their surroundings with two large compound eyes and three other simple eyes located between them. (1)

Typically green or brown and well camouflaged on the plants among which they live, mantis lie in ambush or patiently stalk their quarry. (1)

They use their front legs to snare their prey with reflexes so quick that they are difficult to see with the naked eye. Their legs are further equipped with spikes for snaring prey and pinning it in place. (1)

Their legs are further equipped with spikes for snaring prey and pinning it in place. (1)

The morphology, or body plan, of a praying mantis, is similar to that of many insects. It has six legs, two wings, and two antennae. However, they have some special features which make them unique among insects. (21)

First, they are able to turn their head around as humans can. All other insects cannot turn their heads, their neck is too rigid to allow it. (21)

Second, praying mantids have modified front legs; these front legs are specially designed to catch prey and hold them tightly. These arms are very strong and equipped with pointy spikes to keep a firm hold on the prey. (21)

Most adult praying mantids have wings (some species do not). Females usually cannot fly with their wings,
but males can. (21)

Camouflage and colors, Some mantis species depend on good camouflage to prevent predators from eating them, while others keep a more simple look. Well camouflaged mantids have many projections on their body in the shape of dead leaves, branches, flower petals, or even moss. (21)

Their colors can vary from brown to green, white, pink, yellow, or a mix of all colors. The more straightforwards looks are simply green, brown or sandy colored but without any special modifications. (21)

A praying mantis has 5 eyes, Yes, five eyes! You would think two would be enough, right? Not for a mantis! A mantis has to big compound eyes, the ones you will easily notice. But they also have three smaller eyes located in the middle of their head. Those eyes are used for detecting light while the big eyes are for seeing movement and having depth vision. (21)

Some mantis species have one ear located on their belly Five eyes but one ear? Mantises are crazy! Not every species of mantis has developed the power of hearing, but some did. The auditory organ of a mantis is located in the middle of their belly, just between the last set of legs. Their ear is nothing like ours, as it can only hear loud high-pitched noises. Other sounds can only be felt as vibrations but not as real hearing. (21)

Why would a mantis only want to hear loud high-pitched sounds ???

To avoid bats! When a mantis flies it can be easy prey for bats. At night bats feed on any flying insect and spot them using echolocation. (21)

the diet

these image by fineartamerica.com

the praying mantis or simply mantis is a carnivore. It’s a meat-eating insect that has got many small animals on its menu. Just like its bizarre appearance, mantises are surprisingly ferocious predators one that can take on even small birds including hummingbirds. (99)

The adult praying mantis is strong enough to kill small reptiles. They have a quick launching strategy. (99)

Praying mantis predominantly feed on crickets, grasshoppers, spiders, (monarch) butterflies, beetles, and occasionally other praying mantises. (99)

They are tough predators and are known to consume hummingbirds. Hummingbirds mostly fall trap to mantis when they come to the sugar water to drink. (99)

A big adult praying mantis is most likely to eat smaller individuals. It will supplement its diet with some frogs, leafhoppers, caterpillars, mosquitoes, small birds, and lizards. (99)

Mantises are biotrophic in that they consume carnivorous arthropods and herbivorous arthropods equally. Mantids love to prey on those arthropods that are useful for plants. These animals include butterflies, spiders, wasps, and bees. (99)

the breeding

these image by praying mantis organization

Moths, crickets, grasshoppers, flies, and other insects are usually the unfortunate recipients of unwanted mantid attention. However, the insects will also eat others of their own kind. The most famous example of this is the notorious mating behavior of the adult female, who sometimes eats her mate just after—or even during—mating. Yet this behavior seems not to deter males from reproduction. (1)

The mating behavior of a praying mantis in the wild has been the subject of longstanding debate since the second half of the 19th century yet no convincing studies came to the fore. However, lately, scientists are beginning to take interest as to how mantids mate in the wild. (99)

Adult mantis is known to mate probably in the first week of eclosion. Eclosion is a biological process in which the nymph turns into an adult. (99)

One of the females of S. Carolina copulates nearly five times after reaching adulthood. Sometimes a male mantid can copulate within just 30 hours following eclosion. (99)

For those mantids’ population that inhabits temperate habitats, the mating begins in August and ends in November. This includes mantis living in northern California, Maryland, and Portugal. (99)

Mantis’ eggs however overwinter so they are hatched in April and June the following year. (99)

In some parts of the world, adult male mantises survive all throughout the year, particularly in western Africa and Trinidad. The African mantis’ population appears to spend many months in mating. (99)

Since male mantis does all the hard work such as looking for a female; following pheromones; and approaching her during the daytime, it’s logical to assume that mating occurs somewhere in the daylight hours. (99)

For instance, the Mediterranean mantis (Iris oratoria) and Arizona mantis (Stagmomantis limbata) mount the female around 1200 and 1800 hours. Scientists claim that these are peak mating hours for a praying mantis. (99)

But, not all species mate at noon for there are some such as the South American dead leaf mantis (Acanthops falcata) that mate minutes after the first light of the day i.e. dawn. A few species are observed mating even on full-moon nights although it is too rare to occur. (99)

However, scientists do believe that since researchers have their own limitations that is they just can’t observe mantis mating at night that doesn’t mean all mantises are diurnal. (99)

The Mantis religiosa was once seen approaching the female after sunset. (99)

Worldwide, mantids will communicate their intentions to the mating partner in two main signals; visual, chemical. (99)

Chemical signals involve discharging some kind of chemical to tempt the mating partner. The female mantids will release pheromones when she’s willing to mate. The male will find the female by following these pheromones. (99)

According to the scientists, the M. religiosa female attracts the male from as far a distance as 100 meters away. In species, where females are flightless and live a sedentary lifestyle the pheromones are going to be discharged from far off. (99)

Mantids just don’t rely on visual signals in an attempt to look for their mating partner. (99)

the Behavior

these image by wikipedia organization

Praying mantids have as many senses as we do; sight, smell, taste, feeling, and hearing. However, they mostly depend on sight. Their sense of sight is amazing compared to the abilities of other insects. (21)

They are one of the only kinds of insects that have stereo-vision; it can look with two eyes at the same spot making it possible to judge distances very accurately. Their other senses are weak compared to ours; their sense of smell is mostly limited to smelling specific pheromones that members of their own species emit. (21)

Their sense of hearing is weak but very special in the insect world. Almost all insects cannot hear as we do, they can only sense vibrations. (21)

But praying mantids actually have one ear, in the middle of their abdomen, that can sense the high-pitched tones of a bat! This ear is especially developed to pick up the echolocation calls of bats, the ear cannot hear other tones. (21)

Because of this ear, a flying mantis can hear it when a bat is chasing him in the air. When a flying mantis hears the call of the bat at a close distance, it will suddenly make a turn and let itself drop to the ground. This saves the life of the mantis, as bats are a very common predator of flying insects. (21)

They use their front legs to snare their prey with reflexes so quick that they are difficult to see with the naked eye. Their legs are further equipped with spikes for snaring prey and pinning it in place. (1)

Praying mantis isn’t only the deadliest of predators—it’s the most efficient too. Once it gets hold of the prey the victim is less likely to escape death. (99)

The praying mantis bites the head of the prey then followed by the rest of the body. This is the rule of thumb for eating lunch. (99)

Even so, the female that eats the male during or after mating is thought to consume the male’s head first. If you happen to come across any headless male then it probably is the unlucky mating partner of a hungry female. (99)

There might be a reason as to why mantises are adapted to beheading their mating partner even when they counter no threat. Earlier scientists thought that the ganglion occurs in the mantis’ abdomen (instead of the head) so removing the head possibly enhances fertilization. (99)

They believed that the cannibalizing behavior of a female could be one of the reproductive strategies. However, lately, researchers have rejected this phenomenon. (99)

They said that since the experiments are performed inside the laboratory (artificial environment) the mantis would not be able to demonstrate just the kind of behavior it does in the wild. Let us not forget that the praying mantis is extremely sensitive to any disturbance such as the lights. Mantids just can’t behave in the laboratory the way they do in the natural setting. (99)

Therefore, scientists have yet to decide as to why praying mantis eats the head first.

the conservation

these image by praying mantis organization

Praying mantis predators include, but not limited to, invertebrates, birds, small reptiles such as lizards and frogs, and even spiders. Ants and big hornet species are also known to take on praying mantis. (99)

Wasps such as Tachytes are some of the most successful predators of mantis one that can threaten the lives of adult mantis. Wasps actually love to feed on young mantis. They will eat young mantis by paralyzing their parents. (99)

Praying mantises are not endangered they never were. Some people have long assumed that mantises are not only endangered—they’re also protected by federal or local laws and that it’s illegal to kill one in the wild. (99)

All the above rumors might have possibly originated from Ancient Egyptians and Africans or early civilizations because they consider mantises as a species possessing supernatural powers. (99)

Even so, people worship mantises in some parts of the world. Mantises aren’t poisonous nor do they threaten human fields. In fact, they are considered beneficial insects because they eat harmful bugs. (99)

Therefore, the conservation status of a praying mantis is currently out of the question. (99)
